
**Your character is the vehicle through which you interact with the DM's world. Every character is a combination of class components, backstory, and your personal flair.**

## Who are you?

There are many places to start when creating a character, but the method this book recommends ensures that your character is fun for you, your party, and the DM. This section is tailored primary to my (Awesomecool Dashiell Geiser's) tastes, but most fantasy TTRPGs tables generally function similar to mine.

Communicate and consider with your DM and fellow players what you want the general mood and genre to be for the game. My personal favorite is somewhere between the Resident Evil and Metal Gear series.

First, I recommend choosing a personality for your character which you believe would be fun for you and the rest of the table. Not all character ideas are built for the table, extremely ideological, anti-social, or chaotic characters can easily create schisms at the table which make it difficult to continue the game. Remember that creating a mechanically sound and competent character is quite easy, but making a character that fits your table is more of an investment.

The method which I (Awesomecool Dashiell Geiser) find best for testing whether or not your character will be enjoyable at the table is this:

### Dash's really awesome method of character personality and background and origin and such.

Imagine yourself at a party or other social gathering. You should be able to imagine yourself interacting with your character, and later deciding that although you had a nice talk, you can't see yourself interacting with them much intentionally. They might have been a little too socially awkward, self absorbed, loud, or maybe nothing totally clicked.

This ensures that your character will be able to function well with the other people at your table, but it also means your character won't just be yourself. Never make a character that would not join up with your other party members.

**Next** choose your character's background. Why are they looking out for adventure? Do they seek to indiscriminately aid the good people of the world? Do they require money to pursue a personal goal? Are they looking for a person or magical artifact?

Your character's personality is also important at this step, what led to them behaving they way they do? Why do they react to the world in their own unique way? Think about the answers to some of the following questions:

- Who raised you and how?
    
- What was your proudest moment?
    
- What was your most shameful?
    
- What organizations have you interacted with?
    
- Are you friend or foe to them?
    
- What keeps you going when things get tough?

### Experiences

- Alchemist. You have advantage on Intelligence or Medicine checks to identify a potion, oil, or alchemical substance by sight or smell.
    
- Natural Affinity. You have advantage on Wisdom (Animal Handling) checks with animals from where you grew up.
    
- Strong Swimmer. You have a swimming speed equal to your walking speed.
    
- Sea Legs. You have advantage on checks to avoid falling or being knocked prone on a moving ship or slippery surface.
    
- Weather Eye. After observing the sky and wind for 10 minutes, you can predict natural weather conditions for the next 12 hours with total accuracy, or you know if weather is unnatural.
    
- Streetwise. You have advantage on Charisma checks to gather rumors or find someone willing to buy or sell illegal goods.
    
- One Contact. Work with your GM to establish one specific contact in a city or region of your choice. This person owes you a minor favor and is generally willing to help you with information, shelter, or a small risk.
    
- Bluff. You have advantage on Charisma (Deception) checks to bluff during a game or tense social interaction.
    
- Haggle. you have advantage on Charisma checks to negotiate prices when buying or selling goods.
    
- Fair Price. You automatically know the approximate fair market value of any common trade good (food, cloth, tools, etc.). For rare or exotic items, you have advantage on checks to estimate value.
    
- Handy. You gain proficiency in one Tool of your choice.
    
- Scholar. You learn one language of your choice.
    
- Scout's Eye. When you are the lead traveler (scouting ahead), you have advantage on Perception checks to spot ambushes or hazards.
    
- Experienced Performer. To take this experience you must already have the Hobby Performer experience. You gain proficiency with one musical instrument of your choice, and when you roll below an 8 on the D20 test of a performance check you treat the roll as an 8.
    
- Captivate. When you perform for at least 1 minute, you can cause up to six intelligent creatures who watch you to have disadvantage on Perception checks to notice anything other than your performance, until you stop or they are interrupted.
    
- Sacred Texts. You have advantage on Intelligence checks to recall information about your religion's history, figures, and myths.
    
- Smith. During a long rest, you can create a temporary forge assuming you have access to roughly enough materials.
    
- Worldwise. You have advantage on Charisma checks to understand and navigate rural cultural differences in etiquette or custom.
    
- Squire's Aid. Another creature can reduce the time it takes you to put on heavy armor by half if they use the help action.
    
- Chivalric Court. You have advantage on Charisma checks when interacting with nobles, knights, and other members of the gentry. You are fluent in the etiquette of courts, feasts, and duels.
    
- Watchful. You have advantage on Perception checks while keeping watch during a long or short rest.
    
- Cover Tracks. When you are the lead traveler, your group's tracks are more difficult to follow (creatures tracking you have disadvantage on their checks).
    
- Etiquette. You have advantage on Charisma checks when interacting with nobles, officials, or in formal social situations.
    
- Hobby Performer. You gain proficiency in one musical instrument, additionally you may     use Dexterity or Wisdom instead of Charisma to make a performance check, but you roll 3d4 instead of a d20.
    
- Home Ground. Choose a region type (forest, mountains, swamp, desert, etc.). In that terrain, you cannot become lost except by magical means, and you have advantage on checks to find food, water, or shelter.
    
- Silent Stride. When you move at half speed, you make no sound from footfalls.
    
- Pathfinder. When you travel through wilderness, you and your group move at a normal pace without suffering penalties to stealth or perception.
    
- Befriend the Unnatural. You can use animal handling checks on any creature with less than -3 intelligence.
    
- Alert. Even when you are engaged in another activity while traveling (such as foraging, navigating, or tracking), you remain alert to danger.
    
- Hunter. While tracking other creatures, you also learn their exact number, their sizes, and how long ago they passed through the area.
    
- Mimic. You can accurately mimic the sounds of a creature you have heard. A creature who hears it makes an Insight check against your Mental DC. If the sounds are those produced by a beast, the check has Disadvantage
    
- Guild Membership. Work with your DM to choose a guild or trade organization. Members of that organization recognize you as one of their own and will generally provide basic lodging, meals, local information, or professional advice when reasonable.
    
- Old Debt. Work with your GM to establish one person or family who owes you a significant favor. You may call upon them for reasonable assistance, information, temporary shelter, or a small loan.
    
- Temple Standing. Choose a religion or temple organization. Members of that faith generally provide you food,, shelter, and basic assistance when able, provided you do not act against the interests of the faith.
    
- Military Service. You once served in an organized military force. You have Advantage on checks to identify military ranks, procedures, insignia, and tactics.
    
- Illicit Contact. Work with your GM to establish one fence or black-market trader. This contact is generally willing to buy unusual goods, smuggle items, or people.

## Class and Attributes

This step is what determines the majority of how your character interacts mechanically with the world and in combat.


# Choose a Class

This system has a total of 14 classes to choose from, when choosing a class it is recommended to make your choice based simply on how much fun you think that class sounds to play. Class diversity isn't necessary for a party to function, but in general it is good to have at least one party member from two separate roles. 

Also consider the source of a class's power, a Martial class has no spell casting, a Mystic has some spellcasting meant to supplement their other features, and a Spellcaster's main mode of interaction with the world is through spellcraft.

# Fighting Styles

A @Fighting Style represents the techniques, training, and battlefield habits that define how your character approaches challenges.

All choose a Fighting Style at 1st level. Your choice grants a passive benefit immediately and helps shape your character's role throughout the campaign.

A Fighting Style also grants a permanent Health Bonus, which increases your maximum Hit Points at 1st level and whenever you gain a level.

At 7th level, your Fighting Style grants a special feat. This feat does not count against your Feats Known, does not require a Feat Slot to use, and can be used even if it does not appear on your class feat list or if your class does not use feats.

You must still obey the normal limit of activating no more than one feat on your turn.

### Fighting Style Requirements

Many Fighting Styles require specific equipment or circumstances to gain their benefits.

If a Fighting Style lists a requirement, you only gain its benefits while meeting that requirement.

For example, a character with the @Dueling Fighting Style gains its benefits only while one hand remains free, while a character with the @Polearm Fighting Style gains its benefits only while wielding a weapon with the Reach property.

### Health Bonuses

Each Fighting Style grants either a +2 or +3 Health Bonus.

When you gain a level, increase your maximum Hit Points by the listed amount.

## Ability Scores

@Ability Scores represent your character's capabilities and failings.

To determine your character's ability scores, you purchase 5 of the scores in the graph and assign each to one of your ability modifiers. You have a total of 13 purchasing points, and the lowest a score can go is -1.

| Score | -1  | 0   | 1   | 2   | 3   |
| ----- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cost  | 0   | 1   | 2   | 3   | 5   |

Here is an example of a finished stat spread purchased using all 13 points. It is recommended to have one score with a 3 as this one does.

| Strength  | Dexterity | Intelligence | Wisdom    | Charisma |
| --------- | --------- | ------------ | --------- | -------- |
| 2         | 1         | 2            | 3         | -1       |
| -3 points | -2 points | -3 point     | -5 points | 0 points |

Pay attention to the Physical and Mental modifiers used by the class you chose, those are the scores you want to be the highest. One is usually 3 and the other is usually 2.

A Physical Modifier is Strength or Dexterity. 

A Mental Modifier is Intelligence, Wisdom, or Charisma.

## Race

After working out your background and experiences, you choose a race for your character. Each race gives your character its own special bonuses and determines what they may look like.

When choosing a race, it is recommended to pick one which highlights important parts of your character's traits. For example your big hearted and larger than life Paladin of Sol may be significantly enhanced if you choose to make them an Orc. Your practical naturalist may have a much more graspable character if they are a Moor Elf or Human, leading to meaningful interactions with the world.

# Build Your Character!

You now have everything to start building your character, get a character sheet and follow the next steps.

 1. Fill in each of the 5 Ability scores at the top of your sheet
      
 2. Look at this section of your class's sheet which should look something like this![[Pasted image 20260617173522.png]]
 3. Choose your class's @Fighting Style, this effects the amount of HP you will have.
     
 4. Calculate your level 1 hit points.
     
 5. Write your armor and weapon proficiencies.
     
 6. Mark your saving throw proficiencies by adding the modifier and your proficiency bonus if proficient.
     
 7. Mark your class's skill proficiencies alongside the skills you gained from your background
     
 8. Fill in the 5 Ability Scores you purchased
     
 9. Calculate your skill bonuses by adding the associated ability modifier along with your proficiency bonus if you have proficiency.
     
 10. Mark your character's Physical and Mental Modifiers with an X below them.
     
 11. Write down your Physical and Mental DCs by doing:
 
  Physical DC = 8 + Proficiency Bonus + Physical Modifier

  Mental DC = 8 + Proficiency Bonus + Mental Modifier
  
 12. Write your attack bonuses by doing:

  Melee Attack Bonus = Strength + Proficiency Bonus

  Ranged Attack Bonus = Dexterity + Proficiency Bonus

  Spell Attack Bonus = Mental Modifier + Proficiency Bonus
  
 13. Write down your spellcasting features. If your class gives you spell casting abilities at level 1, the dropdown table on your class sheet shows you what spells you have at what levels and your class tells you what spell list you can draw from.
