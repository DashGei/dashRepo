
Not every challenge can be solved with steel or spellcraft.

As adventurers travel through dangerous lands, explore forgotten ruins, negotiate with powerful figures, and survive hostile environments, they will face countless obstacles that cannot be overcome through combat alone.

This chapter explains the rules used during exploration, travel, social interaction, resting, and other aspects of adventuring.

---

# Ability Checks

Whenever a character attempts an action with a meaningful chance of failure, the Dungeon Master may call for an ability check.

To make an ability check:

1. Roll a d20.
    
2. Add the appropriate ability modifier.
    
3. Add your @Proficiency Bonus if applicable.
    
4. Compare the result to the Difficulty Class (DC).


The Dungeon Master determines which ability and skill apply to the task.

If an ability check's DC is not met, the check fails. For some checks, failing may mean the ability check simply takes a much longer time than desired.

Example:

Churchill the Fighter is running from a hoard of zombies and must push a heavy shelf in front of the doorway. The DC is 15 and Churchill's roll totals 12, but Churchill is very strong and it would be unreasonable for him to be unable to move the bookshelf. Instead of him being totally incapable, Churchill might realize that it will take him around a minute longer to move the bookshelf than he has time left.

# Group Checks

Sometimes the entire party attempts the same task.

When this occurs, the Dungeon Master may call for a group check.

Each participating character makes the appropriate ability check.

If at least half of the group succeeds, the group succeeds.

Otherwise, the group fails.

Group checks are most commonly used for:

- Sneaking as a party.
    
- Traversing dangerous terrain.
    
- Enduring environmental hazards.
    
- Conducting lengthy searches.

---

# Working Together

Two or more characters can cooperate when attempting a task.

If one character assists another and the Dungeon Master determines the assistance is meaningful, the acting character gains @Advantage on the check.

Usually only one creature can provide assistance to a given check.

Typically no more than two players may attempt an ability check, and the DM may require that they wait 10 minutes before they get the chance to make another attempt.

# Social Interaction

Conversations with creatures are resolved through roleplaying first and dice second.

Players are encouraged to describe what their characters say and how they approach a conversation before the DM determines whether they make a roll or not.

When a roll is required, common skills include:

- Deception
    
- Insight
    
- Intimidation
    
- Persuasion


The circumstances of the interaction often matter as much as the result of the roll.

A high result cannot force a creature to act against its nature, beliefs, or self-interest.

# Exploration

Exploration encompasses everything characters do while investigating the world around them.

Examples include:

- Searching rooms.
    
- Interacting with mortals.
    
- Tracking creatures.
    
- Following trails.
    
- Navigating wilderness.
    
- Investigating mysteries.


During exploration, players should describe their actions as specifically as possible.

The more information the players provide, the more information the Dungeon Master can provide in return.

# Adventuring Actions



# @Search Action

Cost: 2 Actions

When you take the Search action, you make a Wisdom check to discern something that isn’t obvious. The Search table suggests which skills are applicable when you take this action, depending on what you’re trying to detect.

#### Search
| Skill       | Thing to Detect                      |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------ |
| @Insight    | Creature’s state of mind             |
| @Medicine   | Creature’s ailment or cause of death |
| @Perception | Concealed creature or object         |
| @Survival   | Tracks or food                       |

---

# @Influence Action

Cost: 1 Action

With the Influence action, you urge a monster to do something. Describe or roleplay how you’re communicating with the monster. Are you trying to deceive, intimidate, amuse, or gently persuade? The DM then determines whether the monster feels willing, unwilling, or hesitant due to your interaction; this determination establishes whether an ability check is necessary, as explained below.

**_Willing._** If your urging aligns with the monster’s desires, no ability check is necessary; the monster fulfills your request in a way it prefers.

**_Unwilling._** If your urging is repugnant to the monster or counter to its alignment, no ability check is necessary; it doesn’t comply.

**_Hesitant._** If you urge the monster to do something that it is hesitant to do, you must make an ability check, which is affected by the monster’s attitude: @Indifferent, @Friendly, or @Hositle, each of which is defined in this glossary. The Influence Checks table suggests which ability check to make based on how you’re interacting with the monster. The DM chooses the check, which has a default DC equal to 15 or the monster’s Intelligence score, whichever is higher. On a successful check, the monster does as urged. On a failed check, you must wait 24 hours (or a duration set by the DM) before urging it in the same way again.

| Ability Check             | Interaction                               |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Charisma (@Deception)     | Deceiving a monster that understands you  |
| Charisma (@Intimidation)  | Intimidating a monster                    |
| Charisma (@Perfermance)   | Amusing a monster                         |
| Charisma (@Persuasion)    | Persuading a monster that understands you |
| Wisdom (@Animal Handling) | Gently coaxing a Beast or Monstrosity     |

---

# @Utilize Action

Cost: 1 or 2 Actions.

Interact with an object that requires more than a normal interaction.

Examples include:

- Activating a mechanism.
    
- Administering a potion.
    
- Manipulating a complex object.

The amount of actions requires depends on the complexity of what you are trying to do, the DM has the final say on how many actions an interaction takes.

# Travel

Adventurers often spend days or weeks journeying between destinations.

During travel, the Dungeon Master determines:

- Travel pace.
    
- Terrain difficulty.
    
- Weather conditions.
    
- Random encounters.
    
- Resource consumption.

Travel provides opportunities for roleplaying, exploration, and unexpected discoveries.

#### Travel Pace

While traveling outside combat, a group can move at a Fast, Normal, or Slow pace, as shown on the Travel Pace table. The table states how far the party can move in a period of time; if riding horses or other mounts, the group can move twice that distance for 1 hour, after which the mounts need a @Short or Long rest before they can move at that increased pace again. The Dungeon Master's Guide has rules that affect which pace you can choose in certain types of terrain.

# Vision and Light

The ability to perceive surroundings depends on available light.

The Dungeon Master determines whether an area is:

- Clear - A clear area such as a lit room or grassy plain, is clear.
    
- Dim - A dim area is @Lightly Obscured, and might be a foggy or poorly lit room
    
- Dark - A creature trying to see in a dark area is Blinded.


Some species possess abilities that allow them to see more effectively in darkness.

Visibility may also be affected by:

- Rain
    
- Smoke
    
- Dense vegetation
    
- Magical effects

# Environmental Hazards

The world contains dangers beyond monsters and enemies.

Examples include:

- Extreme heat
    
- Extreme cold
    
- Deep water
    
- Falling
    
- Suffocation
    
- Disease
    
- Poison

The Dungeon Master determines the effects of these hazards and may call for ability checks or saving throws to avoid them.

# Falling

A falling creature takes 1d6 Physical Damage for every 10 feet fallen, to a maximum of 20d6.

The creature lands @Prone unless a rule states otherwise.

# Resting

Adventurers require rest to recover from injury, exhaustion, and the rigors of travel.

There are two types of rest: @Short Rest and @Long Rest.

---

# Short Rest

A short rest is a period of downtime lasting at least one hour. A creature can only take two short rests per long rest.

During a short rest, characters may:

- Recover class resources that recharge on a short rest.
    
- Benefit from features that activate during a short rest.

Class features determine exactly what resources are recovered.

---

# Long Rest

A long rest is a period of extended downtime lasting at least eight hours.

A character who completes a long rest gains the following benefits:

- Regains all lost hit points.
    
- Regains all expended spell slots.
    
- Recovers resources restored by a long rest.
    
- Reduces their @Exhaustion by 2.

A character can benefit from only one long rest within a 24-hour period.

---

# Exhaustion

@Exhaustion represents accumulated fatigue, grave injury, stress, and hardship.

Being at 0 hit points, some abilities, environmental hazards, and effects can cause Exhaustion.

A creature gains the unconscious condition when it starts its turn with 8 points of Exhaustion. 

A creature dies when it reaches 10 Exhaustion.

Exhaustion is primarily removed through resting.

A short rest reduces Exhaustion by 2.

A long rest reduces Exhaustion by 2.

Some features and abilities may remove additional Exhaustion.

The specific effects of Exhaustion are detailed in the Conditions appendix.

# The Dungeon Master's Role

Not every situation can be covered by a rule.

When unusual circumstances arise, the Dungeon Master decides:

- Whether a roll is required.
    
- Which ability applies.
    
- Which skill applies.
    
- The DC of the task.
    
- The consequences of success or failure.

The rules provide structure, but the Dungeon Master's judgment remains an essential part of the game.

---

# Summary

During adventures, characters explore the world, overcome obstacles, interact with its inhabitants, and survive its dangers.

When success is uncertain:

1. Describe what your character does.
    
2. Determine whether a roll is necessary.
    
3. Make the appropriate check.
    
4. Apply the consequences.
